# Source Types

What counts as a primary or reputable secondary source for the research brief.

## Primary Sources (Prefer These)

- Peer-reviewed papers (PubMed, arXiv with peer-review tag, journal sites)
- Official company case studies (the company's own site or investor materials)
- Government statistics (BLS, Census, OECD, central bank publications)
- Standards documents (ISO, IETF RFCs, W3C, IEEE)
- Direct quotes from named individuals in reputable outlets (FT, WSJ, NYT, Bloomberg, The Atlantic, technical trade press)
- Official product documentation (vendor docs for claims about that vendor's product)

## Reputable Secondary Sources

- Wikipedia for definitional or historical context, with the underlying source verified
- Industry analyst firms (Gartner, Forrester, McKinsey Global Institute) for trend claims
- Technical blogs from named engineers at reputable companies, where the claim is in their domain

## Sources to Avoid

- AI-generated content farms (you can usually tell from the prose pattern)
- Top-N listicles without bylines
- Press releases dressed up as news
- Anonymous expert quotes
- Marketing landing pages making quantitative claims

## When the Numbers Disagree

If two reputable sources publish different numbers for the same fact, both numbers go in the brief. The script writer picks one with the human in the loop, and the disagreement is documented in the brief's Open Questions section.

If no reputable source has the number you need, the script does not assert that number. Hand-wave abstractions are worse than direct uncertainty.

## Direct Quotes

When using a direct quote, copy it verbatim from the source (do not paraphrase and call it a quote). Note the speaker, role, outlet, and date.
